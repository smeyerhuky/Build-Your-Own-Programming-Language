# Origin Story

## The problem that wouldn't go away

For most of Clinton Jeffery's teaching career, the same thing happened every semester: students opened their laptops, tried to install JFlex or BYacc, hit an environment error on the first day, and spent the next two weeks debugging their setup instead of learning compilers.

The knowledge was there. The tools were there. The missing piece was a frictionless path from *zero installed* to *real compiler running*.

Clinton solved that problem for his own students. Then he wrote a book about it.

---

## Phase 1 — The book (2021)

**Build Your Own Programming Language** was published by Packt in 2021. It walked readers through building a complete compiler for **J0** — a small Java subset — using production-grade tools: JFlex for lexing, BYacc/Cup for parsing, Java and Unicon for the runtime.

The book sold well and generated a steady stream of GitHub stars, forum questions, and emails from readers who had gotten through it but wanted more: more exercises, more tooling, more interactive feedback.

Two patterns emerged clearly:

1. **The setup friction problem was universal.** Readers from Europe, Asia, and South America hit the same environment bugs that New Mexico Tech students hit.
2. **Enterprises were reading it too.** DevOps engineers building configuration DSLs. Data engineers building query languages. Policy teams building rule engines. They were not students — they needed production tooling, not a textbook.

---

## Phase 2 — The wiki and onboarding bundle (2026)

The GitHub repository was restructured with an **OKF (Open Knowledge Format)** wiki: structured, LLM-navigable documentation covering every chapter, concept, and tool. An onboarding bundle (`/startup/`) was added to take a reader from zero tools to a running J0 compiler in five guided phases.

The OKF structure turned out to solve a second problem nobody had anticipated: **LLM-assisted learning**. When a student pasted an error message into an LLM and pointed it at the onboarding bundle, the LLM could give specific, accurate help — because the context was structured and machine-readable.

This was the insight that turned the book into a startup idea.

---

## Phase 3 — The startup hypothesis (2026)

The hypothesis: **compiler construction knowledge is trapped in books and university courses. LangCraft will make it accessible, interactive, and production-ready.**

Three concrete bets:

1. **Education bet.** An interactive online platform — with a cloud sandbox, LLM assistant, progress tracking, and certificates — would convert the large audience of developers who *want* to learn compiler construction but find the book too self-directed.

2. **Enterprise bet.** A commercial toolchain — J0 as a DSL foundation, with a VS Code plugin, LSP server, package registry, and enterprise SSO — would serve the growing market of teams building internal DSLs.

3. **Platform bet.** Once LangCraft owns the toolchain, it becomes the platform on which the next generation of DSL-native companies build. Think Stripe for payments, but for programming language infrastructure.

---

## Phase 4 — Incorporation and seed (planned)

LangCraft Inc. is planned for **Delaware C-Corp incorporation in Q3 2026**. The founding team is assembling. A pre-seed round of **$500K–$1M** will be used to:

- Build the cloud sandbox and interactive course platform (MVP in 6 months).
- Hire a founding engineer and a developer relations lead.
- Launch the free tier and begin growing a developer community.

A **seed round of $2–4M** will follow in 2027, contingent on MVP traction, to fund the enterprise product and first sales hire.
